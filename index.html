Failures of tech in education in developing countries

Seth Youngstrom
10/16/2022

No Laptop Left Behind details the issues behind technology driven education efforts in low income countries. The main conclusion drawn being that while supplying internet and devices to schools can help it will not do anything without solving more deep rooted underlying issues such as lack of motivated teachers and administrators as well as extreme lack of funds or support staff to keep the devices running. Technology driven education efforts are nothing new, other past methods detailed include using radio to broadcast lessons from some of the world's top teachers and professors to “bring the world to the classroom”. Another effort in the 1950s included television, “ John F. Kennedy convinced Congress to authorize $32 million for classroom television programs”, this led to American Samoa basing its entire school system on televised instruction. The problem with these efforts is said to be this, “real education involves two-way interaction, while broadcast media is only one-way”, and while many would like to believe different rigorous studies consistently point otherwise. The primary claim of all the tech driven efforts being that if students are given access to devices then their naturally curious minds will use it to learn, though that has been difficult to prove with more sources citing that students are more likely to use them for games or entertainment over any learning software. These devices need training and guidance for them to be of any use and in a system where the teachers do not care and do not have the support staff to keep devices running the tech ends up helping no one.

Primary issue with lack of support staff

Seth Youngstrom
10/16/2022

The hope behind device integration into classrooms is that it makes education better for students and teachers alike, however it ends up making the jobs for already exhausted teaching staff harder. Adding unreliable computers to the equations meant that “teachers have to design lesson plans involving computers, they also had to write low-tech backup plans in case of technology failures”. Without critical IT and training professionals to ensure that the computers given would function and the teachers would be able to effectively use them then they became a liability. Teachers were forced to come up with contingency plans, likely weakening both the high and low tech lessons as less time was spent on each one. A large part of using any teaching tool in a classroom relies on consistency and reliability. Computers in a high humidity, hot and dirty environment are prone to breaking and schools cannot afford to have IT staff on constant employment to fix these issues so much of the tools they are given to teach cannot be properly relied on. Technology cannot be unloaded into schools without addressing more base systematic issues and expect positive results, or any at all.
